The defining differential equation for SHM is: \begin{equation} \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + \omega^2 x = 0 \end{equation} The general solution is: \begin{equation} x(t) = A \cos(\omega t) + B \sin(\omega t) \end{equation} Where \( A \) and \( B \) are determined by initial conditions. The angular frequency \( \omega \) is related to mass \( m \) and spring constant \( k \) by: \begin{equation} \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \end{equation} Velocity \( v(t) \) and acceleration \( a(t) \) follow as: \begin{equation} v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = -A\omega \sin(\omega t) + B\omega \cos(\omega t) \end{equation} \begin{equation} a(t) = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -\omega^2 x(t) \end{equation} \section{Graphical Representation} \begin{figure}[h] \centering \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{shm_plot.png} \caption{Displacement vs Time for SHM with \( A = 1 \), \( \omega = 2 \pi \).} \label{fig:shm} \end{figure} The graph in Figure \ref{fig:shm} shows a perfect sinusoidal wave illustrating the periodicity and symmetry of SHM. \section{Tabular Data} Table \ref{tab:data} shows displacement values for a simulated SHM system with \( A = 1 \), \( \omega = \pi \), over time. \begin{table}[h] \centering \caption{Displacement over time} \begin{tabular}{ccc} \toprule Time (s) & Displacement (m) & Velocity (m/s) \\ \midrule 0.0 & 1.000 & 0.000 \\ 0.5 & 0.000 & -3.142 \\ 1.0 & -1.000 & 0.000 \\ 1.5 & 0.000 & 3.142 \\ 2.0 & 1.000 & 0.000 \\ \bottomrule \end{tabular} \label{tab:data} \end{table} \section{Conclusion} SHM provides critical insights into physical systems that exhibit periodic behavior.
